# Chapter 1 — The 24-Hour Constraint

Most people do not lose because they lack information. They lose because they never enter a real execution window.

The 24-Hour Ship Sprint exists to force one outcome: **a shipped offer with active distribution and a live attempt at first revenue.**

## Why time pressure works

Without constraints, your brain invents fake work:
- logo tweaks
- domain rabbit holes
- "just one more feature"
- endless tool comparisons

A hard deadline removes those options. You stop asking what is ideal and start asking what ships.

## The Two-Block Structure

This method runs in two blocks:

### Block A (Hours 0–12): Build + Launch
You decide on one offer, package it, publish a sales page, attach checkout, and ship launch assets.

### Block B (Hours 12–24): Sell + Close
You distribute hard, handle objections, follow up, and push for first payment.

If Block A fails, Block B has nothing to sell.
If Block B is skipped, Block A is just a hobby project.

Both blocks are mandatory.

## The Rule of One

For this sprint, you only get:

1. One audience
2. One painful problem
3. One offer promise
4. One primary CTA
5. One checkout link

Every extra option lowers conversion and increases delay.

## Product selection in plain language

A sprint-safe product is one that meets all three:

- **Painful:** The problem is expensive, embarrassing, or urgent.
- **Purchasable now:** Buyer already spends money in this category.
- **Deliverable quickly:** You can produce first version in under one day.

Examples of good sprint products:
- Social content pack for local service businesses
- Cold outreach script pack for freelancers
- Landing page teardown service with fixed turnaround
- Niche prompt vault for a specific job role

Examples of bad sprint products:
- Apps requiring complex auth and backend dependencies
- Anything needing regulatory approval before sale
- Broad "all-in-one" offers with unclear outcomes

## Offer sentence framework

Use this sentence before writing any page copy:

> We help **[specific audience]** get **[specific outcome]** in **[specific timeframe]** without **[specific pain/risk]**.

If you cannot complete this sentence with concrete terms, your offer is not ready.

## The anti-perfection operating mode

Your working standard during the sprint:
- Clear enough to buy
- Credible enough to trust
- Fast enough to ship

Not:
- Perfect design
- Full automation
- 100-page docs before first customer signal

Perfection comes after proof.

## What counts as "shipped"

A sprint is only considered shipped when all are true:

- Sales page is publicly accessible
- Payment link works end-to-end
- Delivery method exists (download/email/manual)
- Launch post is live
- At least 20 outbound touches sent

If one of these is missing, you are still in prep mode.

## First-sale psychology

People don't buy because your product is complete.
People buy because your promise is clear and your next step is obvious.

Your job is to reduce friction:
- Short headline
- Strong promise
- Simple offer stack
- Visible price
- Single CTA

Complexity is not authority. Clarity is authority.

## Chapter 1 Action Checklist

Before moving to Chapter 2, complete this list:

- [ ] I chose one audience.
- [ ] I defined one painful problem.
- [ ] I wrote the offer sentence.
- [ ] I set fixed pricing tiers.
- [ ] I accepted "ship now, improve after proof" mode.

If these are not done, stop and do them now.

The rest of the sprint depends on this foundation.

# Chapter 2 — Choose the Right Product in 45 Minutes

If you choose the wrong product, execution speed won’t save you. This chapter gives you a strict filter so you can decide fast and move.

## The 3-Fit Filter

Score each idea from 1–5 on:

1. **Pain Intensity** — Does this problem hurt enough to pay now?
2. **Buyer Liquidity** — Does this audience currently spend on this type of solution?
3. **Delivery Speed** — Can you deliver the first version inside 24 hours?

Any idea scoring below 10 total is cut.

## Fast Candidate Generation (10 minutes)

Generate 5 offer ideas max. Keep each idea in one line:

- Audience
- Problem
- Outcome
- Format

Example:
"Local roofers — inconsistent social posting — 30-day post kit — delivered as templates + captions"

Do not research for an hour. Generate, score, cut.

## Red Flags (Instant Disqualifiers)

Kill any idea that requires:
- multi-step onboarding before value
- custom app logic to deliver the core result
- legal/regulatory approvals before sale
- integrations that may break before launch

Sprint offers should be robust under pressure.

## Offer Sentence Test

Write one sentence for each finalist:

> We help **[audience]** get **[outcome]** in **[time]** without **[pain]**.

If the sentence sounds vague, the offer is vague.

## Price Fit Test

Run one of these fixed anchors:
- Entry digital product: $19–$49
- Template/productized bundle: $49–$99
- High-touch review/service add-on: $149–$299

If you cannot justify one anchor clearly, reframe outcome.

## 45-Minute Decision Sprint

- Minute 0–10: generate 5 ideas
- Minute 10–25: score each with 3-Fit
- Minute 25–35: write offer sentence for top 2
- Minute 35–45: pick one and lock it

No re-opening after minute 45.

## Chapter 2 Action Checklist

- [ ] I generated max 5 candidate offers
- [ ] I scored each by Pain/Liquidity/Speed
- [ ] I removed weak ideas (<10)
- [ ] I selected one offer and locked it
- [ ] I set initial price anchors

Next: package the offer into tiers buyers can understand instantly.

# Chapter 3 — Offer Architecture (Lite / Pro / Sprint)

A good offer removes decision fatigue. People should understand what they get in seconds.

## Why 3 Tiers Work

Three tiers create contrast:
- **Lite** anchors affordability
- **Pro** becomes obvious value
- **Sprint** captures high-intent buyers

Most buyers should choose Pro. Build your stack around that.

## Tier Design Rules

### Lite (Entry)
- one core asset
- low friction
- fast checkout

### Pro (Main)
- everything in Lite
- practical implementation assets
- strongest value-per-dollar

### Sprint (Premium)
- everything in Pro
- limited high-touch component
- scarcity by capacity, not fake urgency

## Example Stack

### Lite — $29
- Playbook PDF

### Pro — $79
- Playbook PDF
- Prompt Vault
- Launch Templates
- Objection Scripts

### Sprint — $199
- Pro bundle
- 1 teardown slot (limited)

## Outcome Framing

Each tier description should answer:
1. What result does this unlock?
2. What do I receive instantly?
3. What is the fastest path to implementation?

Avoid feature language. Use outcome language.

## Risk Reversal (without hype)

Use practical confidence statements:
- "Built for immediate execution"
- "Designed for one focused 24-hour sprint"
- "No fluff, all templates included"

Avoid income guarantees.

## Offer Block Template

Use this exact layout on page:

- Tier name
- Price
- 3–5 bullets
- CTA button
- one-line "best for" descriptor

Example "best for":
- Lite: "Best for solo builders starting now"
- Pro: "Best for buyers who want copy/paste execution"
- Sprint: "Best for buyers who want direct feedback before launch"

## Chapter 3 Action Checklist

- [ ] I defined Lite/Pro/Sprint clearly
- [ ] Pro is strongest value tier
- [ ] Tier copy is outcome-first
- [ ] I removed vague feature jargon
- [ ] I added clear "best for" lines

Next chapter: build the page + checkout in 3 hours.

# Chapter 4 — Build the Page + Checkout in 3 Hours

This chapter is about one thing: getting to a live URL with a working payment link.

## The 3-Hour Build Sprint

### Hour 1: Message and structure
Build these sections only:
1. Hero (outcome + timeframe)
2. Problem (why they are stuck)
3. Offer stack (what’s included)
4. Pricing (Lite/Pro/Sprint)
5. FAQ
6. CTA

If a section doesn’t increase checkout intent, remove it.

### Hour 2: Checkout wiring
- Create Stripe products for each tier
- Generate checkout links
- Put real links on each CTA button
- Test checkout in live mode with small real transaction if possible

### Hour 3: Delivery path
- Create success page
- Add access instructions on success page
- Send confirmation email copy (manual or automated)
- Add support contact

## Sales Copy Rules

- Headline must state outcome + speed
- Bullets must answer: “What do I get right now?”
- Pricing must be visible without scrolling too far
- CTA must be action-based (“Get Instant Access”)

## Required Conversion Elements

- Social proof placeholder (or founder note if no testimonials yet)
- Urgency line (launch pricing ends at [time])
- Low-risk statement (clear support/refund policy)

## Stripe Setup Checklist

- [ ] Product names match landing tiers
- [ ] Checkout descriptions are clear
- [ ] Success URL routes correctly
- [ ] Customer email captured
- [ ] Confirmation email includes delivery links

## Chapter 4 Action Checklist

- [ ] Live page published
- [ ] Checkout links functional
- [ ] Success page ready
- [ ] First test transaction validated

No page + no checkout = no sprint. Ship this before writing more content.

# Chapter 5 — Launch Assets (Posts, DMs, Objection Scripts)

Your launch assets are your sales force for the first 24 hours.

## Asset Stack (minimum)

1. One long-form launch thread/post
2. Three short posts
3. Two cold DM variants
4. Two follow-up messages
5. One objection response sheet

## Long-Form Launch Post Structure

1. Hook: "I built this in 24 hours"
2. Pain: "Most people overplan and never ship"
3. Mechanism: "One audience, one offer, one checkout"
4. Offer: what’s inside + tiers
5. CTA: checkout link + deadline

## Short Post Templates

- "Build in 12. Sell in next 12. That’s the sprint."
- "Most people need a clock, not another course."
- "If you want the exact process + templates: [link]"

## DM Variant A (Direct)

"Quick one, [Name] — I packaged a 24-hour sprint to help builders ship an offer and push first sale immediately. Want the link?"

## DM Variant B (Personalized)

"Saw your recent post about [topic]. I built something tactical that helps go from planning to shipped + selling in one day. Can I send it over?"

## Follow-Up Sequence

- Follow-up #1 (2-4h): "Happy to send checkout if useful."
- Follow-up #2 (deadline): "Launch pricing closes tonight."

## Objection Handling

### “I need to think”
"Totally fair — what part is unclear: product choice, pricing, or distribution?"

### “Too expensive”
"If this helps close one sale, it pays for itself and becomes reusable weekly."

### “Not now”
"No pressure — this is best when you’re ready for one focused execution day."

## Chapter 5 Action Checklist

- [ ] Long-form launch post published
- [ ] 3 short posts published
- [ ] 30 DMs sent
- [ ] Follow-up wave executed
- [ ] Objections tracked and reused in copy

# Chapter 6 — Close the First Sale (Hours 12–24)

This is the revenue block. You are no longer building. You are closing.

## Close Window Priorities

1. Respond fast to warm leads
2. Send direct checkout links
3. Use deadline clarity
4. Follow up with intent

## The 4-Step Close Sequence

### Step 1: Qualify quickly
Ask one question:
"Are you trying to ship your own offer this week?"

If yes, move to checkout. If no, park politely.

### Step 2: Re-state outcome
"This gives you the exact process + assets to ship in 12h and push for first sale in the next 12h."

### Step 3: Offer direct path
"Want Lite, Pro, or Sprint? I’ll send the right checkout link now."

### Step 4: Deadline close
"Launch pricing closes tonight at [time]."

## Follow-Up Cadence

- T+2h: short check-in
- T+6h: clarify objection
- T+10h: deadline reminder

Do not spam. Keep messages brief and specific.

## Common Deal Killers

- Too much explanation instead of link
- Delayed replies (>2h)
- Weak CTA ("let me know")
- No urgency anchor

## What counts as a close attempt

A close attempt includes all three:
- direct offer reminder
- checkout link
- time-bound reason to act now

If one is missing, it’s not a close attempt.

# Chapter 7 — Post-Sale Proof Loop

Your first sale is not the finish line. It is the beginning of the conversion engine.

## The 24-Hour Post-Sale Loop

1. Deliver immediately
2. Ask for quick feedback/testimonial
3. Turn feedback into proof snippet
4. Publish proof in next launch post
5. Re-run sprint with improved copy

## Delivery Script

"You’re in. Here’s your access link and quick-start steps. If you want, I can also point you to the exact section to run first."

## Testimonial Request Script

"Once you review it, can you send a one-line note on what was most useful? I may include it (with your permission) in future launch posts."

## Proof Formats

- One-line testimonial
- Screenshot of positive buyer reply
- Before/after execution result
- Mini case snippet: "Shipped in 1 day, sent 40 DMs, got first buyer"

## Chapter 7 Checklist

- [ ] Buyer got access instantly
- [ ] Feedback requested within 24h
- [ ] At least one proof asset captured
- [ ] Proof reused in launch copy

# Chapter 8 — Weekly Repeat System

The sprint becomes powerful when repeated weekly.

## Weekly Cadence

- Day 1: run sprint on current offer angle
- Day 2: review objections and conversion data
- Day 3: improve page and scripts
- Day 4: relaunch
- Day 5: follow-up and close

## What to Improve Each Cycle

1. Headline clarity
2. Offer positioning
3. Pricing stack
4. DM open rate
5. Follow-up speed

## KPI Benchmarks

- Reply rate target: 15–30%
- Checkout click-through: 5–12%
- First-week close rate: 1–5%

If below benchmarks, tighten audience and simplify promise.

## Chapter 8 Checklist

- [ ] Weekly sprint date scheduled
- [ ] Data from prior cycle logged
- [ ] One major variable changed
- [ ] Relaunch executed

# Chapter 9 — Operating Rules (Never Break)

1. One offer per sprint.
2. No rebuilding mid-sprint.
3. No design rabbit holes before checkout works.
4. Distribution volume beats overthinking.
5. First sale > first logo polish.

## Failure Modes to Avoid

- Endless idea switching
- Waiting for perfect tools
- Posting once and calling it a launch
- Not following up warm leads

## Success Rule

You are judged by shipped offers and close attempts, not by preparation quality.

## Final Checklist

- [ ] Offer shipped
- [ ] Checkout tested
- [ ] Assets published
- [ ] Outbound sent
- [ ] Follow-ups done
- [ ] Proof captured

Run it. Measure it. Improve it. Repeat.
